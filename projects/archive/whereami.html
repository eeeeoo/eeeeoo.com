Where Am I: Anthropological Approaches to Design and Architecture
Ethnographic Research, Service Design
1 week

Credits
Prof. Justin Armstrong

“Where am I?” is a question we often ask ourselves, even though we might not be lost geographically. It is a question that examines our relationship between ourselves and our surroundings. While we feel insecure when we are lost in space, it is interesting how we get accustomed to spaces, how we navigate without maps, and mind map spaces. People get comfortable with spaces, but how do we reach that phase?

This is a project of pedestrian signage developed by incorporating anthropological thinking into design to navigate the Wellesley College campus. I aimed to translate my understanding of Wellesley into real experiences for people by trying to solve the problem of navigating within campus by using my experience, knowledge, and available tools in Wellesley.

Goal

"Where Am I" hopes to improve people’s navigation and accessibility to areas by mediating the strange and familiar, the new and old, by using the iconic Wellesley lamppost with a new signage that uses digital services to scan QR codes to know their current location.

Design Process

Brainstorming

Questions I considered

  • Using an object already made to make an other object, does it become a thing?

Background Research

Wellesley's campus covers 500 acres and as many landscapes. The landscape has always been central to the identity of Wellesley College and to the experience of the students. Henry Fowle Durant, founder of the college, believed that "young women should be educated in the midst of beauty."

Wellesley College is divided not only geographically by east and west campus, but also the dorms and the humanities and sciences are also divided.

Lamppost Research

Not Objects, But Things

The Wellesley lamppost, first introduced in the 1920s, continues to represent an iconic element and constant variable in the college environment regardless of the changes in nature. The lamppost acts beyond the function as a lighting fixture, and has transformed into

  • a tourist landmark, where people take photos
  • an inspiration for merchandise
  • an interior object (inside the Science Center)
  • a tattoo to show school pride

Lampposts have become a reification of Wellesley College and its ideologies. The history of lamppost is also contained within the Wellesley College student’s souls.


The Physical Lamppost

I used the information to understand the appropriate height of the signs that would be attached to the lamppost. Assuming the majority of the students would identify their gender as female, I took the average height for women in the United States to create an approximate placement for the eyes and hands for the signs in ratio to the height of the lamppost.

Concepts

Wayfinding

The project takes wayfinding, a method using a visual language such as signs to support seamless journeys in a perplexing environment.

Space into a place

Signs acknowledge that space embodies a certain value and recognition to be labeled and founded by others

Controlling, but people trust signs

Or are we even making our own decisions? How do we trust signs?

Abstractions

Signs and maps are abstractions, not a representation of the territory

It's all about the power we give in

If the lampposts were placed in a different context, would the meaning still be same or would the space in Wellesley be the same?

Visual Design

A shift from vernacular architecture damages the natural beauty and culture, so I tried to keep the vernacular style of Wellesley and the lamppost by using a specific color schemes. The “Wellesley Blue” color and class colors are important components that reflect the visual identity of Wellesley college.

Lamppost QR
Bus Stops

For the bus stop signs, the bus information uses the four class colors to represent the four different bus stations and the container that holds the signs appropriate the design of the lampposts. The bus stop signs also has multiple sides like the lamppost, so the design of the lamppost and sign would blend together.

Final Design


Accessibility

QR codes for individuals with cognitive impairments. Focused on the physical conditions of female students in order to place the signs in ratio of the height of the lamppost.

Resistibility

Signs are not placed at the bottom of the lamppost, because of the snowy weather in Wellesley. Signs are to be water resistant, so qr codes can be scanned and the bus information is readable.

Readability

Use Helvetica due to larger x-height and wide proportions. Helvetica works well under low vision conditions and it contrasts clearly against the background from desired distances. Qr codes replace textual directions, which support people with limitations in language.

Challenges

More Etic Approach & User Research

It is important to put aside subjective opinions. Understanding the culture of Wellesley and the problems with the navigation occurred due to my experience at Wellesley.

Development Costs

Requires thorough selection of signs, because not all lamps can have qr codes

Integrity of Design
